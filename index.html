Consonance & Dissonance

Intervals which are consonant tend to sound harmonious, pleasant, and agreeable. Dissonant intervals tend to sound harsh, unpleasent, and unagreeable.

But how do we know what is consonant and what is dissonant?

Your ears are actually great calculators. The more "complex" the ratio (or specifically the denominator), the more dissonant.

Context generally defines what is consonant/dissonant, but here's a general guideline:
Perfect consonances: Unisons, Octaves, Perfect 4ths, Perfect 5ths
Imperfect consonances: Major/Minor 3rds, Major/Minor 6ths
Sharp & soft dissonances: Depends on musical context

Chords (Triads)

Simple definition: A chord is when 3 or more distinct notes are played simultaneously. We define chords by their root note, and the intervals relative to that root note, which give us the chord quality/chord type. A chord triad, or just triad, is a chord with 3 notes.
Chord Triads (With C Roots)
Name/Quality Intervals Notes Play
Major (maj) Root, Major 3rd, Perfect 5th C, E, G
Minor (min) Root, Minor 3rd, Perfect 5th C, Eb, G
Diminished (dim) Root, Minor 3rd, Diminished 5th C, Eb, Gb
Augmented (aug, +) Root, Major 3rd, Augmented 5th C, E, G#
There are many more types of chords, such as 7th chords, but these are simplest chords that you'll see out there.

Scales/Keys

A scale is a sequence of notes, or intervals, based off a root note. The most common scales in "Western" music are heptatonic, which means they have 7 notes per octave. One way to define a scale is using a sequence of stacking whole and half steps.
A key is a set of notes, typically a scale, that act as the harmonic basis for a composition.
Simple Scales (Starting at C)
Name Intervals Notes Play
Major (Ionian mode) W-W-H-W-W-W-H C, D, E, F, G, A, B, C
Natural Minor (Aeolian mode) W-H-W-W-H-W-W C, D, Eb, F, G, Ab, Bb, C
There are also the Harmonic Minor (W-H-W-W-H-m3/A2-H) and Melodic Minor (W-H-W-W-W-W-H). Some composers will use the melodic minor while ascending and the natural minor while descending.
There exist many more scales/keys/modes, even ones that are not heptatonic, that all have their own unique sound, but there are outside the scope of this talk.

Diatonic Chords

We say a chord is diatonic to a key if all the notes that comprise that chord are notes from that key. For example, a C major chord is: C, E, G and a C major key has the notes: C, D, E, F, G, A, B. This means that the C major chord is diatonic in the C major key.
It is also useful when discussing keys to talk about the key or scale degree, which is just the nth note in that key. For example in the C major key, 1st = C, 2nd = D, 3rd = E, etc. We typically use Roman numerals to indicate this.
Now, for both the major and minor key, if we start at each scale degree and use that note for the root of a chord, we can see what their diatonic chords are.
We've already done this for the 1st of the C major key, which has a diatonic triad of the C major chord.

Functional Harmony & Chord Progressions

The function or a chord/scale degree is the relationship that it has to the tonal center (or root) of a scale/key, typically the first degree.
We call the first degree of a key the tonic.
The tonic usually seen as the most stable chord in a key. As we move farther away from the tonic, we create dissonance and tension that wants to be resolved back to the tonic.
We can define functions for each scale degree of a key, but ultimately these are not set in stone.
Common Functions of Scale Degrees
Degree Name/Function Play
I Tonic, tonal center, home
II Supertonic, relative of the subdominant
III Mediant, relative of dominant, counterrelative of tonic
IV Subdominant/Predominant
V Dominant, tension, unstable
VI Submediant, relative of the tonic
VII Leading Tone
A chord progression is a sequence of chord that typically act as the harmonic foundation for a song.
Knowing what we now know, we can start to use chord progressions as a tool for analyzing and creating music.
